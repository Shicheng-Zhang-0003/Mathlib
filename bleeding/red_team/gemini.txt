### Task 1: Assembly-Level Auditing (The `-S` Deep Dive)

We can't just trust GCC or Clang's `-O3` flag blindly. Modern compilers do incredible things, but they can also make bone-headed vectorization choices, like spilling ymm registers to the stack inside loops or failing to utilize Fused Multiply-Add (FMA) instructions because of strict IEEE-754 safety rules.

* **Our Target:** Compile our newly added Horner-evaluated minimax polynomials and AVX2 matrix routines directly to assembly using `-S -masm=intel`.
* **What we're hunting for:**
* **FMA Generation:** Look for `vfmadd213pd`, `vfmadd132pd`, or `vfmadd231pd` instructions. If you see scalar multipliers followed by additions (`vmulsd` + `vaddsd`), it means the compiler is playing it safe. We must force its hand using `#pragma GCC optimize("associative-math")` or manual fast-math flags restricted solely to these localized TU (translation unit) scopes.
* **Register Spilling:** Check the loop bodies. Are we pushing/popping vector registers to and from the stack pointer (`rsp`)? If we see `vmovapd [rsp + ...], ymmX`, our matrix kernel is register-starved. We need to restructure our vector variables to keep the hot values inside the `ymm0` through `ymm15` silicon.



### Task 2: Denormal and Subnormal "Zero-Kill" Verification

In high-performance math libraries, **subnormal numbers** (floating-point numbers extremely close to zero, where the leading significand digit is zero) are a silent performance killer. Processing a subnormal on modern CPUs can take up to **100x longer** because the CPU handles them via slow microcode assists rather than hardware ALU pipelines.

* **Our Target:** Ensure our algorithms handle subnormals elegantly or fail gracefully without grinding our pipeline to a halt.
* **What we're hunting for:**
* **Branch-Free Flush-to-Zero (FTZ):** Ensure our `_graphics` profile cleanly supports or coexists with FTZ and Denormals-Are-Zero (DAZ) hardware flags.
* **Underflow Bounds:** In our minimax approximations (like `ml_log` and `ml_exp`), verify that extremely small numbers do not accidentally trigger an implicit division by zero or throw NaN when they underflow. We need to audit our Cody-Waite range reduction bounds to handle these seamlessly.



### Task 3: The IEEE-754 Boundary Gauntlet (Fuzzing the Fringes)

Now that our math is optimized, we must make sure it is unbreakable. We are going to point our fuzzing harness (`fuzz_boundary_gauntlet.c`) at the new polymorphic symbols (`_scientific`, `_graphics`, `_fixed`) and feed them "toxic" inputs.

* **The Input Deck:** We will feed our functions:
* `+0.0` and `-0.0` (ensure signed zero behaves exactly as expected—e.g., `ml_log(-0.0)` must return $-\infty$, not NaN).
* $\pm\infty$ (Infinity).
* Quiet NaNs, Signaling NaNs.
* The transition points right at our interval reduction boundaries (e.g., exactly at $\sqrt{2}/2$, where range reduction changes branches).


* **The Verdict:** If any function throws a segmentation fault, enters an infinite loop, or returns a value outside of our exact ULP error contract, the build is rejected, and we fix the boundary branch.

### Task 4: Strict Thread-Safety and Reentrancy Audit

Since we unified our namespaces and allowed dynamic profile swapping, multiple threads might be calling `ml_sin_scientific` and `ml_sin_graphics` simultaneously.

* **Our Target:** Verify that MathLib has **zero mutable global state**.
* **What we're hunting for:**
* Any `static` variables inside functions used for caching or state tracking. If they exist, they must be converted to thread-local storage (`thread_local` or `__thread`), or completely refactored out into pure functions.
* Audit the dynamic dispatcher's initialization. Ensure the `ml_init_cpu_dispatch()` routine is completely idempotent or protected by a lightweight atomic spinlock so that concurrent library initializations do not cause a race condition on the CPU capability registers.
